Breast Cancer Detection

Breast cancer is a significant concern in India, ranking as one of the most prevalent cancers affecting women across the country. The incidence rates have been rising steadily, highlighting the growing burden of breast cancer on the population. It is crucial to understand the multifaceted impact of breast cancer in India, including its psychological, social and economic effects on patients and their families. Recognizing the diverse range of risk factors such as genetic predisposition, lifestyle choices and environmental influences is essential for developing targeted prevention strategies. The importance of early detection cannot be overstated, as it significantly improves treatment outcomes and survival rates. Symptoms of Breast Cancer

  • A new lump or thickened area in the breast or underarm
  • Unexplained changes in breast size or contour
  • Skin on the breast may appear dimpled or puckered
  • Sudden inversion, flattening, or unusual changes in the nipple
  • Ongoing pain in the breast or nipple that does not subside
  • Clear, red, or bloody discharge from the nipple, unrelated to breastfeeding
  • Swelling or an increase in the size of one breast
  • Breast skin may become red, warm, or have an orange-peel texture
  • Unusual visibility of veins on the breast surface

Diagnosis

  • The diagnosis of breast cancer typically begins with a clinical breast exam, followed by imaging tests such as mammography or ultrasound to detect any abnormalities. If suspicious areas are found, a biopsy is performed to collect tissue samples for laboratory analysis. This helps determine the presence of cancer cells and provides information on the type and stage of cancer, which is crucial for developing an appropriate treatment plan. Advances in diagnostic techniques, including genetic testing, also aid in identifying individuals at higher risk and guiding personalized treatment approaches. Early detection significantly improves the chances of successful treatment and survival.

As of 2023, breast cancer remains the most common cancer among women globally, with approximately 2.3 million new cases and around 670,000 deaths each year. The risk of developing breast cancer increases with age, though it can affect women of all ages, including those without specific risk factors beyond age and gender. The disease imposes significant psychological, social, and economic burdens, affecting patients' quality of life and contributing to substantial healthcare costs. Despite advancements in treatment and early detection, breast cancer remains challenging to manage due to its complexity and variability in response to treatment. The World Health Organization underscores the importance of early detection and preventive measures to improve outcomes and reduce incidence rates.
